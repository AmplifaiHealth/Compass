Section 01

The Core Philosophy

The COMPASS framework integrates two fundamentally different kinds of athlete health data — each answering a different clinical question that the other cannot.

⌚ Readiness Score via API

  • Pre-computed by wearable API provider
  • Integrates HRV, resting HR, sleep, ACWR
  • Baseline-adjusted by the provider
  • Delivered as a 0–100 readiness score

Answers: "Is this athlete's body ready to perform?"

Baseline establishment and longitudinal normalisation are handled entirely by the wearable API provider (e.g. SpikeAPI). COMPASS consumes the output score — no internal wearable baseline is required.

VS

🌡️ Thermography COMPASS Baseline

  • Contralateral asymmetry index
  • Asymmetry trend over time
  • Focal vs diffuse patterns
  • Regional tissue stress

Answers: "Is there a developing problem in this specific body region?"

Thermal baselines are athlete-specific and established directly within COMPASS across an initial series of scans. This is the only signal requiring internal baseline management.

The Founding Insight: These two questions are not the same question. A wearable cannot tell you that the right hamstring is inflamed. A thermogram cannot tell you whether the athlete slept well. The power of combining them is not that they overlap — it is that they do not.

Why the Deceptive Readiness Zone matters most: Injuries rarely happen to athletes who feel terrible. They happen most often to athletes who feel great, train hard, and cross a threshold that their tissue had been approaching invisibly for days or weeks. This zone is where thermography's early-warning capability saves careers.

How the COMPASS Score Is Calculated

The zone an athlete falls into is determined first — that is non-negotiable. The score is then computed within that zone based on how far from the threshold boundary both signals sit. This two-step approach preserves the clinical meaning of each zone while adding continuous resolution within it.

Score Formula
Step 1 — Determine Zone (R = Readiness, T = Thermal Risk, both 0–100)
R ≥ 50 and T < 50 → Safe Zone
R < 50 and T < 50 → Recovery Zone
R ≥ 50 and T ≥ 50 → Deceptive Readiness
R < 50 and T ≥ 50 → Danger Zone
Step 2 — Compute Score within Zone
Safe (76–100) 76 + 24 × avg( (R−50)/50 , (50−T)/50 )
Recovery (51–75) 51 + 24 × avg( R/50 , (50−T)/50 )
Deceptive (26–50) 26 + 24 × ( 0.3×(R−50)/50 + 0.7×(100−T)/50 )
Danger (0–25) 25 × ( 0.3×R/50 + 0.7×(100−T)/50 )
⚖️
Why Thermal Is Weighted 70% in Risk Zones

In the Deceptive and Danger zones, the thermal signal carries 70% of the within-zone score, versus 30% for readiness. This reflects the clinical asymmetry at the heart of the framework: when tissue stress is elevated, how the athlete feels is an unreliable guide. The score is deliberately pulled down by thermal risk even when readiness is high — ensuring an athlete who feels great but has significant asymmetry cannot score in the upper half of the Deceptive band.

Section 05

The Role of Personalisation

The readiness axis requires no baseline work from COMPASS — that is handled entirely by the wearable API provider. Personalisation within COMPASS is therefore focused exclusively on the thermal axis: establishing each athlete's individual thermal baseline so that asymmetry thresholds reflect what is elevated for them, not for the population.

⌚
Wearable Readiness: Zero Baseline Burden

COMPASS receives a pre-computed, baseline-adjusted readiness score (0–100) directly from the wearable API provider (e.g. SpikeAPI). Individual normalisation, HRV trend analysis, sleep scoring, and ACWR calculation are all performed upstream. COMPASS consumes the output — no wearable data storage, baseline accumulation, or longitudinal modelling is required on the platform side.

Thermography baselines must be built within COMPASS because they are institution- and equipment-specific. A meaningful personal thermal baseline requires a minimum series of scans captured under consistent conditions.

Section 05B

Individual Baseline SD Classification

Applied once an athlete's personal thermal baseline is established (6 or more scans under standardised conditions). Each body region acquires a personal mean and SD from scan history. Subsequent readings are classified relative to this individual norm — a materially more sensitive and specific reference frame than population thresholds alone.

🎯
Why Individual SD Outperforms Population °C Thresholds

Individual-referenced models outperform population norms across all sports monitoring biomarkers (Saw et al. 2016). Machine learning models trained on personal baselines achieve AUC 0.84 vs. 0.61 for group-norm models (Claudino et al. 2024). The COMPASS 1.5 SD alert threshold corresponds to approximately 0.4°C for major muscle groups — directly aligning with the Arnaiz-Lastras (2019) prevention protocol threshold, confirming both frameworks draw the boundary at the same underlying physiological signal.

Section 06

Temporal Fusion Strategy

The two data streams operate on fundamentally different timescales. The framework explicitly models this asymmetry rather than engineering around it.

6.1 Thermal Prior Decay

The most recent thermography assessment acts as a persistent contextual prior that modifies the wearable-based readiness signal on a day-by-day basis. The weight applied to this prior decays as time since the last scan increases. A single uniform half-life of 7 days applies regardless of zone — this keeps the model simple, auditable, and easy to explain to clinical staff.

📐
Modeling Assumption — Not Literature-Derived

The 7-day half-life is a clinical approximation based on standard exponential decay, chosen for simplicity and auditability. No peer-reviewed study directly quantifies how long a thermographic asymmetry reading remains predictively valid after capture. This parameter should be treated as institution-adjustable: clinical leads are encouraged to revise it as longitudinal outcome data accumulates within their own cohort.

All Zones
7 days
Modeling assumption · Adjustable by clinical leads
⏰
Scan Overdue Threshold: 7 Days

Once 7 days have elapsed since the last thermography scan, the thermal prior has decayed to 50% of its original weight — the minimum meaningful clinical threshold. The system suspends thermal risk scoring and flags an overdue scan, rather than issuing zone classifications on residual data with insufficient clinical weight.

6.3 Handling Wearable Data Gaps

Unlike thermography (which decays gracefully), wearable data gaps require suspension — not downweighting. HRV from several days ago carries no reliable predictive weight for today's readiness.

✅
Gap 0–2 Days: Flagged Assessment

Zone classification proceeds using last known wearable values, visibly marked as "estimated — based on data from [date]." Practitioners receive a sync reminder but are not blocked from acting on the output.

🚫
Gap >2 Days: Assessment Suspended

No readiness score generated. No zone classification issued. If a valid thermal prior is available, a thermal-only advisory is produced. The daily output reads: "Zone assessment unavailable — wearable sync required. Last confirmed zone: [zone] on [date]."

Section 07

Clinical Decision Support System

The CDSS translates zone classifications into outputs practitioners can act on, across three distinct time horizons.

7.1 Daily Output — Traffic Light System

Each morning, the system generates a traffic-light classification incorporating overnight wearable data and the current weighted thermal prior.

🟢

Green — Safe Zone

Both signals confirm readiness. Full training load appropriate. No clinical intervention indicated.

🟡

Amber — Recovery Zone

HRV suppressed, no thermal alert. Reduce volume or intensity. General recovery protocol.

🟠

Amber — Deceptive Readiness

Readiness normal, thermal alert active. Modified loading; region-specific soft tissue assessment. Do NOT increase intensity.

🔴

Red — Danger Zone

Both signals alarming. Rest or mandatory clinical review. Not overridable — requires documented practitioner response.

Why Amber Is Not a Single State: Conventional monitoring systems collapse Recovery Zone and Deceptive Readiness Zone into one amber. This destroys the most important clinical distinction the framework makes. An athlete in Recovery Zone amber needs rest. An athlete in Deceptive Readiness Zone amber needs region-specific soft tissue assessment — the systemic readiness signal is actively misleading.

7.2 Rule-Based Physiotherapy Triggers

⚡
Trigger: Soft Tissue Assessment

Thermal asymmetry index for any single region exceeds 1.5 SD above personal thermal baseline AND ACWR is above 1.3 → generate a soft tissue assessment recommendation for that specific region.

🏥
Trigger: Medical Review

Athlete has been in Danger Zone for more than 5 consecutive days without improvement in either sub-score → generate mandatory medical review recommendation.

📅
Trigger: Scan Overdue

Thermal prior decay falls below minimum meaningful threshold and athlete is not in Safe Zone → escalating scan overdue alert. Classifications after this threshold flagged as "wearable signal only — thermal context unavailable."

7.3 Design Principle: Transparency & Explainability

Every output generated by the system must include an explanation of which signals drove it and why. Machine learning models that generate black-box predictions are not appropriate for the recommendation layer — even if they demonstrate strong predictive accuracy in isolation.

Section 08

Zone Trajectory: Movement Matters

A single zone reading is a snapshot. What drives clinical urgency is movement: the rate and direction of change on each axis is as important as the current position.

Illustrative Zone Trajectories — 14-Day Athlete Monitoring Window

Strong

1.6 — Thermography Scan Frequency & Monitoring Protocols

The literature supports increasing scan frequency when thermal asymmetry is detected, but does not prescribe specific day-interval values. The intervals used in Section 6.2 are framework design decisions directionally supported by the studies below.

Strong

Gómez-Carmona et al. (2020)

Journal of Sport Rehabilitation, 29(8), 1222–1227
Prospective study in 24 professional soccer players. Daily thermography-based monitoring during preseason reduced injury incidence from 0.63 to 0.25 injuries per player and days lost from 156 to 14 days. Supports high-frequency scanning when thermal risk is elevated. Directly supports more frequent scanning in Deceptive Readiness and Danger zones.
Moderate

Arnaiz-Lastras et al. (2019)

PMC — Pilot Study (complementary screening)
Twice-weekly thermography (48 hours post-match) in professional soccer. A prevention protocol was triggered when asymmetry exceeded 0.4°C. Muscle injuries reduced from 11 to 4 over the monitored period. Directly supports the twice-weekly cadence for Return-to-Sport Rehab and elevated-risk zones.
Emerging

Frontiers in Sports and Active Living (2024)

Systematic Review — IRT & Internal Load in Athletes
Systematic review of 13 studies (n=231 athletes, Tier 2+). IRT parameters pre/post exercise are sensitive to skeletal muscle overload and fatigue. Supports periodic scanning as a practical non-invasive load monitoring tool, though optimal intervals remain unspecified in the literature.

1.7 — Thermographic Population Normative Values

Foundational studies establishing what constitutes a "normal" contralateral thermal asymmetry in healthy athletes. These normative values anchor the population-based classification framework used in Section 05A and define the population fallback thresholds active during athlete onboarding.

Strong

ThermoHuman Research Group (2022)

ThermoHuman Applied Research — High Performance Center Niedersachsen, Germany
Normative thermal asymmetry dataset from 950 healthy high-performance athletes without pain, injury, or influence factors. Global mean asymmetry: 0.004 ± 0.66°C, confirming bilateral thermal homeostasis. Most stable regions (chest ± 0.24°C SD; lumbar ± 0.26°C SD; vastus medialis ± 0.27°C SD). Most variable regions: heel (±3.73°C SD), wrist (±0.72°C SD). 0.3°C established as the lower alert boundary; 0.6°C as the treatment action boundary. Directly underpins the No Finding (<0.3°C) and Moderate (0.30–0.49°C) tiers of the population framework.
Strong

Uematsu et al. (1988) — Part 1: Normal Values

Journal of Neurosurgery, 69(4):552–555
Foundational clinical thermography study establishing normative asymmetry values from 90 asymptomatic control subjects using computerised thermometric scanning. Region-specific normal values: forehead 0.18 ± 0.18°C, leg 0.27 ± 0.20°C, foot 0.38 ± 0.31°C. Global asymptomatic ceiling: ≤ 0.38°C. Reproducibility validated across sessions. Establishes the population-level SD framework: the per-region SD from this dataset is the reference against which the SD-based tier classifications are calibrated.
Strong

Uematsu et al. (1988) — Part 2: Clinical Application

Journal of Neurosurgery, 69(4):556–561
Applied the normative values from Part 1 to 144 patients with low-back pain and sciatica. When asymmetry exceeded 1 SD above the normal mean of homologous regions: PPV = 94.7%, specificity = 87.5% for detecting pathological nerve root impingement. Establishes that 1 SD above the mean is a clinically meaningful threshold with strong diagnostic performance — the theoretical basis for the SD-based tier classification in Section 05B. A 2.0 SD personal threshold (Severe tier) would represent an even higher-confidence signal.

1.8 — SD Classification Framework in Sports Monitoring

Evidence supporting the use of standard deviation tiers — rather than fixed absolute values — as the authoritative classification system once an individual baseline is established. These studies underpin the COMPASS 1.5 SD alert threshold and the broader SD tier framework in Section 05B.

Strong

Plews, D.J. et al. (2013) — SD Tier Consensus

International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, 8(5):461–470
Establishes the widely-adopted SD tier framework for individual monitoring in sport: <0.5 SD = within normal variation; 1.0 SD = meaningful departure (training modification flag); 1.5 SD = clinically important; ≥2.0 SD = extreme deviation requiring clinical review. Applied here to thermography because the same intra-individual departure logic applies: the question is not "how far from population average?" but "how far from this athlete's personal norm?" Directly supports the 1.0 / 1.5 / 2.0 SD tier boundaries used in Section 05B.
Strong

Saw, A.E., Main, L.C., & Gastin, P.B. (2016) — Individual vs. Population Reference

British Journal of Sports Medicine, 50(3):192–200
Intra-individual variability in HRV, sleep, and wellness scores is more sensitive to training load and fatigue than absolute values. Individual-referenced thresholds outperform population-referenced thresholds across all wellness biomarkers. Below 1 SD from personal baseline = normal fluctuation; above 1.5 SD = clinically important departure. Directly validates the principle that individual SD thresholds supersede population °C thresholds — the foundational logic of the Section 05B framework.
Emerging

Claudino et al. (2024) — Individual Baselines in ML Models

British Journal of Sports Medicine
Machine learning models trained on individual athlete baselines significantly outperformed models trained on group norms for injury risk prediction (AUC 0.84 vs. 0.61). 28–35 days of training data required for reliable individual baseline establishment (consistent with the 4–6 scan, 3–5 week COMPASS onboarding protocol). Quantifies the performance gap between individual and population reference frames: the SD-based individual framework is not just conceptually superior — it is measurably more predictive.

1.9 — Thermal Asymmetry Risk Stratification Studies

Studies directly establishing which absolute asymmetry values (°C) correspond to clinically meaningful injury risk levels. These anchor the Moderate and High population-based tiers where the literature provides most specific threshold evidence.

Moderate

Marins, J.C.B. et al. (2014)

International Journal of Morphology, 32(3):769–776
Thermal body pattern study in healthy Brazilian adults establishing region-specific normative asymmetry values and risk gradients. Asymmetry ≥ 0.3°C warrants clinical attention in major muscle groups. Values approaching 0.4°C indicate meaningfully elevated injury risk. Recommended for clinical investigation and contextualisation with load data when exceeded. Anchors the lower boundary of the Moderate tier (0.30°C) and confirms the transition zone toward the High tier prevention threshold approaching 0.4–0.5°C.
Strong

Gómez-Carmona et al. (2020) — 0.5°C Clinical Threshold

Journal of Sport Rehabilitation, 29(8):1222–1227
Asymmetry >0.5°C identified 83% of subsequent soft tissue injuries before symptom onset (n=24 professional players). Detection occurred an average 8.2 days prior to clinical presentation. Daily thermography protocol reduced injuries from 0.63 to 0.25 per player, and days lost from 156 to 14. Definitively establishes 0.5°C as the lower boundary of the High tier — and confirms that the corresponding SD value (≈ 1.85 SD) sits firmly within the 1.5–1.99 SD COMPASS alert band, validating the calibration between both frameworks.
Moderate

Arnaiz-Lastras et al. (2019) — 0.4°C Prevention Protocol

PMC — Pilot Study (Complementary Screening)
Twice-weekly thermography (48 hours post-match) in professional soccer. Prevention protocol triggered when asymmetry exceeded 0.4°C. Muscle injuries reduced from 11 to 4 over the monitored period — a 64% reduction. Establishes 0.4°C as an operational prevention threshold. Critically, 0.4°C ≈ 1.5 SD for major muscle groups, confirming that the COMPASS 1.5 SD alert threshold and this literature-based 0.4°C threshold are drawing the line at the same physiological signal.
