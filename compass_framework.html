Section 01

The Core Philosophy

The COMPASS framework integrates two fundamentally different kinds of athlete health data — each answering a different clinical question that the other cannot. Its architecture is grounded in the theoretical argument by Kalkhoven, Watsford & Impellizzeri (2021) that systemic training load metrics cannot, by themselves, provide a quantitative indication of tissue-level injury risk.

⌚ Readiness Score via Spike · Daily

  • Collected daily from Spike wearable
  • Daily averages: avg HRV, resting HR, sleep quality, ACWR
  • Baseline-adjusted by Spike before delivery
  • Delivered as a single 0–100 readiness score

Answers: "Is this athlete's body ready to perform?"

Spike collects raw sensor data continuously and publishes one daily summary per athlete — end-of-day averages for HRV, resting HR, sleep score, and ACWR. These daily statistics are what flow into the readiness score delivered to COMPASS. Baseline establishment and longitudinal normalisation are handled entirely by Spike. COMPASS consumes the output score — no internal wearable baseline is required.

VS

🌡️ Thermography via NUR sensor

  • Contralateral asymmetry index
  • Asymmetry trend over time
  • Focal vs diffuse patterns
  • Regional tissue stress

Answers: "Is there a developing problem in this specific body region?"

Thermal baselines are athlete-specific and established directly within COMPASS across an initial series of scans. This is the only signal requiring internal baseline management.

The Founding Insight: These two questions are not the same question. A wearable cannot tell you that the right hamstring is inflamed. A thermogram cannot tell you whether the athlete slept well. The power of combining them is not that they overlap — it is that they do not.

Theoretical Foundation

Kalkhoven, Watsford & Impellizzeri (2021) demonstrated that training load measures cannot provide a quantitative indication of whether particular load progressions will increase or decrease injury risk at the tissue level — making the case for a mechanistic, tissue-specific layer above and beyond systemic load monitoring. COMPASS operationalises that argument directly: the Systemic Readiness Score captures the internal load domain (HRV, sleep, ACWR), while the Thermal Asymmetry Index adds the tissue-specific spatial resolution that no systemic metric can reach.

Kalkhoven JT, Watsford ML, Impellizzeri FM. A critique of the use and misuse of training load in team sports: towards a fuller understanding of the relationship between external and internal load. Sports Med. 2021;51(3):469–479. PMC7534945 ↗

Section 02

The COMPASS Framework

Plotting Systemic Readiness against Maximum Thermal Asymmetry creates a two-dimensional space in which every athlete occupies one of four clinically distinct zones — each carrying a genuinely different meaning and requiring a genuinely different clinical response.

IOC Load Consensus — Capacity Gap Model

The IOC Consensus on load management (Soligard et al. 2016, BJSM) defines injury risk as a function of the gap between an athlete's load and their capacity: when accumulated load exceeds the athlete's capacity to absorb it, injury risk rises. COMPASS zones map directly onto this load-capacity relationship — combining systemic capacity (Readiness Score) with local tissue capacity (Thermal Asymmetry Index) to characterise the precise nature of any capacity gap present.

Soligard T, et al. How much is too much? International Olympic Committee consensus statement on load in sport and risk of injury. Br J Sports Med. 2016;50(17):1030–1041.

Why the Deceptive Readiness Zone matters most: Injuries rarely happen to athletes who feel terrible. They happen most often to athletes who feel great, train hard, and cross a threshold that their tissue had been approaching invisibly for days or weeks. This zone is where thermography's early-warning capability saves careers.

⚖️
Equal Weighting of Readiness and Thermal Signals

The shipped framework combines the readiness (R) and thermal (T) clearances on a single equal-weight plane — each contributes 50% to the score (READINESS_THERMAL_WEIGHT = 0.5). The thermal weight is one tunable constant: raising it would make tissue asymmetry pull the score down more aggressively relative to perceived readiness, but the default treats the two axes symmetrically. Crucially, zone identity is set independently by the 2×2 quadrant — so an athlete who feels ready (R high) but carries significant asymmetry (T ≥ 50) still lands in Deceptive regardless of the weighting, and the continuous score reflects exactly how far each signal sits from its boundary.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1

Collect raw signals

Spike collects continuous sensor data and publishes one daily summary per athlete. Each day's output includes avg HRV resting HR sleep quality ACWR — these daily statistics are forwarded to the Spike API to generate the readiness score. The thermography camera captures a bilateral IR scan and returns the per-region left/right deltas across all scanned regions.

2

Compute R and T scores

The wearable API delivers a pre-normalised R score (0–100) — COMPASS does not perform any readiness modelling itself. For the T score (0–100), every bilateral region's delta is standardised against its effective reference (personal baseline shrunk toward the population) into a z deviation, mapped to 0–100 via the severity anchors, and the worst region drives the scan T — a single unified path, no method switch.

3

Classify zone via 2 × 2 matrix

R and T are each thresholded at 50. The combination of above/below determines which of the four zones applies: R ≥ 50, T < 50 → Safe; R < 50, T < 50 → Recovery; R ≥ 50, T ≥ 50 → Deceptive; R < 50, T ≥ 50 → Danger.

4

Combine on the equal-weight plane

R and T are converted to signed clearances from their 50 boundary and combined with equal weight (0.5 / 0.5) into one continuous value. There is no zone-specific weighting and no per-zone band: the same plane applies everywhere, so the score moves smoothly as either signal changes.

5

Calculate final COMPASS score

The combined clearance is scaled and clamped to a single value in 0–100. The result encodes both axes: Safe readings land 50–100, Danger 0–50, and the two mixed zones each span 25–75. Zone identity (from Step 3) and the continuous score are reported together.

Steps 1–5 produce the COMPASS score and zone assignment. The pipeline then continues through an AI reasoning layer (Stage F) to practitioner outputs (Stage G) — detailed in Section 07. The full equations behind Steps 2–4 are in § 3.1.

Section 04

The Role of Personalisation

The readiness axis requires no baseline work from COMPASS — that is handled entirely by the wearable API provider. Personalisation within COMPASS is therefore focused exclusively on the thermal axis: establishing each athlete's individual thermal baseline so that asymmetry thresholds reflect what is elevated for them, not for the population.

⌚
Wearable Readiness: Zero Baseline Burden — Powered by Daily Spike Data

COMPASS receives a pre-computed, baseline-adjusted readiness score (0–100) directly from the Spike API. Spike collects raw sensor data continuously and publishes one daily summary per athlete — end-of-day averages for HRV, resting HR, sleep quality, and ACWR. These daily statistics are processed entirely by Spike: individual normalisation, HRV trend analysis, sleep scoring, and ACWR calculation all happen upstream. COMPASS consumes the output — no wearable data storage, baseline accumulation, or longitudinal modelling is required on the platform side.

Thermal Baseline Establishment

Thermography baselines are built per region within COMPASS because they are institution- and equipment-specific. There is no minimum-scan gate: every scan produces a classification. With no personal history the reference is purely the population (μ_pop = 0, σ_pop = 0.40°C); as scans accumulate, each region's personal mean/SD is blended in via empirical-Bayes shrinkage, so personalisation strengthens continuously rather than switching on at a fixed scan count. 04B.1 — Thermal Risk Score (T Score) Calculation

Each bilateral region's left/right temperature delta is converted to a normalised T Score (0–100) before entering the COMPASS fusion formula. This score represents the thermal risk axis of the two-axis matrix. A single unified path is used for every region and every athlete — there is no separate population/SD method and no switch-over scan.

📐
Worst-Region Aggregation

Each region produces its own T value; the scan T Score is the maximum single-region T across all scanned regions — the single worst-performing region drives the score. No averaging is applied.

Step Computation Notes
1 · Shrunk reference σ_eff = √((n·σ_i² + n0·σ_pop²)/(n+n0))
μ_eff = (n·μ_i + n0·μ_pop)/(n+n0)		 Personal (μ_i, σ_i) over n scans blended toward population (μ_pop = 0, σ_pop = 0.40°C) with prior strength n0 = 5.
2 · Standardise z = |Δ − μ_eff| / σ_eff Standardised bilateral asymmetry against the shrunk reference.
3 · Severity score T = clamp((z − 1.0)/(3.0 − 1.0) × 100, 0, 100) Anchors: z = 1.0 → T 0 · z = 2.0 → T 50 · z = 3.0 → T 100.

With no personal history (n = 0) the reference is purely the population, so the population mode maps absolute deltas directly: 0.40°C → T 0 · 0.80°C → T 50 · 1.20°C → T 100. As scans accumulate, the personal mean/SD progressively replace the population reference via the shrinkage weight n/(n + n0) — at n = 5 the personal and population terms carry equal weight. The change is continuous, not a step at any fixed scan number.

🔄
Continuous Personalisation — No Switch-Over Scan

There is no parallel computation or hard cut-over at any scan count. From the very first scan the same formula runs; the only thing that changes over time is how much weight the athlete's own history carries relative to the population prior. For athletes with atypically high or low thermal variability, the accumulating personal baseline yields a progressively more accurate — and often lower — T Score, without any discontinuity in zone assignment.

Section 7.4

Return-to-Play: Thermal Eligibility Module

COMPASS replaces time-based and subjective RTP decisions with a continuous, per-region thermal eligibility model. Every athlete in the Deceptive Readiness or Danger zone is monitored across all 89 measured body regions. A region side is declared RTP-eligible only when its asymmetry severity has stayed below the concern band (Moderate) on consecutive scans — providing objective, anatomy-specific clearance evidence rather than a single composite score.

The same thermal scan that detects hidden tissue stress before injury becomes the most objective tool available for governing the return-to-play decision. Individual baselines established during regular scanning carry directly into the eligibility assessment — an athlete's own thermal norm, not a population reference, defines when a region is cleared.

Section 7.4.1

The Eligibility Criterion — Two Consecutive Non-Concern Scans

A region side is classified as RTP-eligible once it has at least 2 qualifying scans and neither of its two most recent readings flagged that side at Moderate or Elevated severity (z > 2.0). A single normal reading is insufficient; the check spans the last two scans so clearance reflects stable normalisation, not a transient fluctuation. With fewer than 2 qualifying scans the region returns N/A (insufficient history). Clearance is evaluated independently for each side of a bilateral pair — only the affected (hotter) side carries the concern.

Why a severity category, not an absolute °C? The live clearance threshold is the same z-based severity that drives the COMPASS T Score — Moderate begins at z > 2.0. Because that z is computed against each region's shrunk reference (the athlete's own thermal norm once history accumulates, the population SD before then), RTP adapts per athlete and per region rather than applying one fixed population °C value. This keeps return-to-play decisions consistent with the rest of the framework and removes the need for per-region calibration tables. (The clinical literature on inter-limb symmetry — Hildebrandt et al.; IACT guidelines — informs why ~0.8°C in population terms is a sensible concern boundary, but it is not hard-coded as the threshold.)

Section 7.4.2

  • Undisclosed secondary involvement — elevated asymmetry at a region not identified in the original injury report, potentially representing a co-occurring or pre-existing stress load the athlete has not reported.

Section 7.4.3

Contralateral Limb Monitoring

Injured athletes compensate by redistributing load to the contralateral limb. COMPASS bilateral scanning tracks both limbs simultaneously throughout rehabilitation. The contralateral limb is not a passive reference — it is an active monitoring target with its own independent eligibility status.

For each of the 43 bilateral region pairs, COMPASS computes the left/right temperature delta at the region and tracks it across every scan:

delta[scan_n] = |left_region.asymmetry[n] − right_region.asymmetry[n]|

This per-pair time series reveals three clinically distinct patterns:

  • Diverging delta — one limb improving, one worsening; classic compensatory load pattern requiring load redistribution.
  • Converging delta — both limbs normalising simultaneously; bilateral symmetry restoring; RTP clearance approaching.
  • Parallel elevation — both limbs elevated; systemic or bilateral stress, not a unilateral injury pattern.

Rising asymmetry on the contralateral limb during rehabilitation signals compensatory overload. The athlete cannot perceive this stress — it is invisible until tissue failure occurs.

1.7 — Thermographic Population Normative Values

Foundational studies establishing what constitutes a "normal" contralateral thermal asymmetry in healthy athletes. These normative values anchor the population-based classification framework used in Section 05A and define the population fallback thresholds active during athlete onboarding.

Strong

ThermoHuman Research Group (2022)

ThermoHuman Applied Research — High Performance Center Niedersachsen, Germany
Normative thermal asymmetry dataset from 950 healthy high-performance athletes without pain, injury, or influence factors. Global mean asymmetry: 0.004 ± 0.66°C, confirming bilateral thermal homeostasis. Most stable regions (chest ± 0.24°C SD; lumbar ± 0.26°C SD; vastus medialis ± 0.27°C SD). Most variable regions: heel (±3.73°C SD), wrist (±0.72°C SD). 0.3°C established as the lower alert boundary; 0.6°C as the treatment action boundary. Directly underpins the No Finding (<0.3°C) and Moderate (0.30–0.49°C) tiers of the population framework.
Strong

Uematsu et al. (1988) — Part 1: Normal Values

Journal of Neurosurgery, 69(4):552–555
Foundational clinical thermography study establishing normative asymmetry values from 90 asymptomatic control subjects using computerised thermometric scanning. Region-specific normal values: forehead 0.18 ± 0.18°C, leg 0.27 ± 0.20°C, foot 0.38 ± 0.31°C. Global asymptomatic ceiling: ≤ 0.38°C. Reproducibility validated across sessions. Establishes the population-level SD framework: the per-region SD from this dataset is the reference against which the SD-based tier classifications are calibrated.
Strong

Uematsu et al. (1988) — Part 2: Clinical Application

Journal of Neurosurgery, 69(4):556–561
Applied the normative values from Part 1 to 144 patients with low-back pain and sciatica. When asymmetry exceeded 1 SD above the normal mean of homologous regions: PPV = 94.7%, specificity = 87.5% for detecting pathological nerve root impingement. Establishes that 1 SD above the mean is a clinically meaningful threshold with strong diagnostic performance — the theoretical basis for the SD-based tier classification in Section 05B. A 2.0 SD personal threshold (Severe tier) would represent an even higher-confidence signal.

1.8 — SD Classification Framework in Sports Monitoring

Evidence supporting the use of standard-deviation tiers — rather than fixed absolute values — for individual monitoring. These studies informed the decision to use a z-based severity model in Section 05B; the live anchors (z = 1 / 2 / 3) are set in code, not taken verbatim from any one study.

Strong

Plews, D.J. et al. (2013) — SD Tier Consensus

International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, 8(5):461–470
Establishes the widely-adopted SD tier framework for individual monitoring in sport: <0.5 SD = within normal variation; 1.0 SD = meaningful departure (training modification flag); 1.5 SD = clinically important; ≥2.0 SD = extreme deviation requiring clinical review. Applied here to thermography because the same intra-individual departure logic applies: the question is not "how far from population average?" but "how far from this athlete's personal norm?" Background support for a z/SD-based severity model; COMPASS sets its own anchors (z = 1 / 2 / 3) in code rather than adopting these exact tier values.
Strong

Saw, A.E., Main, L.C., & Gastin, P.B. (2016) — Individual vs. Population Reference

British Journal of Sports Medicine, 50(3):192–200
Intra-individual variability in HRV, sleep, and wellness scores is more sensitive to training load and fatigue than absolute values. Individual-referenced thresholds outperform population-referenced thresholds across all wellness biomarkers. Below 1 SD from personal baseline = normal fluctuation; larger departures = clinically important. Supports referencing each athlete to their own baseline — in COMPASS the personal mean/SD is blended toward the population via shrinkage, rather than switching over from a population threshold.
Emerging

British Journal of Sports Medicine

1.10 — Training Load Theory & the Capacity Gap Model

The theoretical underpinning of COMPASS's dual-axis design: why systemic load monitoring alone is insufficient for tissue-level injury risk detection, and how the IOC load-capacity model maps onto the four COMPASS zones.

Strong

Kalkhoven, J.T., Watsford, M.L., & Impellizzeri, F.M. (2021)

Sports Medicine, 51(3):469–479 · PMC7534945 ↗
Demonstrated that training load measures — whether external (GPS, ACWR) or internal (HRV, RPE) — cannot provide a quantitative indication of whether particular load progressions will increase or decrease injury risk at the tissue level. Argued that a mechanistic, tissue-specific layer is required above and beyond systemic load monitoring. Directly motivates the COMPASS dual-axis architecture: the Thermal Asymmetry Index provides the spatially-resolved tissue signal that no systemic metric can reach, operationalising the theoretical gap this paper identifies.
Strong

Soligard, T. et al. (2016) — IOC Consensus on Load in Sport

British Journal of Sports Medicine, 50(17):1030–1041
International Olympic Committee consensus statement defining injury risk as a function of the gap between an athlete's accumulated load and their capacity to absorb it: when load exceeds capacity, injury risk rises. Established the load-capacity framework as the theoretical lens for interpreting all monitoring data. COMPASS zones map directly onto this model — Safe (no gap), Recovery (systemic capacity eroded), Deceptive Readiness (hidden local-tissue capacity gap invisible to systemic metrics), Danger (critical dual-axis gap) — providing a principled clinical interpretation for each zone beyond the score label alone.
