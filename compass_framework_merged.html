Overview

What the NUR platform is used for

COMPASS is the clinical framework behind the NUR platform by Amplifai Health. It is deployed across three interlocking use cases — each drawing on the same dual-signal architecture this document walks through.

Use case 01

Injury-risk prevention

Daily monitoring of uninjured athletes to surface deceptive readiness — the hidden state where the wearable looks normal but tissue stress is already building. COMPASS is built to flag this before symptoms appear.

Use case 02

Return to play

For athletes in the Deceptive or Danger zone, COMPASS tracks all 89 measured regions and clears each side only when its asymmetry stays below the concern band across consecutive scans — anatomy-specific evidence, not a single composite score.

Use case 03

Load management

The wearable recovery score — built from HRV, sleep, resting HR and workload — drives daily, athlete-specific load guidance, letting staff modulate intensity against an objective recovery state rather than feel alone.

The big picture

Two signals, one readiness call

Injuries rarely strike athletes who feel terrible. They strike athletes who feel great — cleared by every systemic metric — while a single muscle is already stressed. Wearables describe the body as a whole; they can't see one inflamed hamstring. Thermography can see the hamstring, but says nothing about whether the athlete is recovered enough to train.

COMPASS reads both. The Thermal-Risk score (TR) answers is any tissue stressed? — built from a bilateral thermography scan. The Recovery score (R) answers is the body recovered? — read from the athlete's wearable. Fused, they produce a zone and a 0–100 score, plus a per-region return-to-play verdict.

The founding insight: these two questions are not the same question. A wearable cannot tell you the right hamstring is inflamed; a thermogram cannot tell you whether the athlete slept. The power of combining them is not that they overlap — it is that they don't.

Theoretical foundation

Kalkhoven, Watsford & Impellizzeri (2021) showed that systemic training-load measures cannot, on their own, indicate whether a given load progression raises or lowers injury risk at the tissue level — making the case for a mechanistic, tissue-specific layer above systemic monitoring. COMPASS operationalises that argument: the recovery score captures the internal-load domain (HRV, sleep, ACWR), while the thermal axis adds the tissue-level spatial resolution no systemic metric can reach.

Kalkhoven JT, Watsford ML, Impellizzeri FM. A critique of the use and misuse of training load in team sports. Sports Med. 2021;51(3):469–479. PMC7534945 ↗

The rest of this document follows a single scan through that machine, one step at a time. We'll track one region the whole way: a runner's right hamstring, which today reads a touch warm.

The journey

The nine steps, end to end

Two branches run in parallel. A five-step thermal pipeline turns the scan into a single risk score; the wearable delivers a recovery score ready-made. They fuse at the COMPASS step, and two further steps govern how the system learns and clears the athlete. The map below is the route — each stop is a section.

STEP 1 The thermal signal

The bilateral difference, Δ

  • The body is broadly symmetric — a healthy left and right sit within a fraction of a degree of each other.
  • So COMPASS asks "how different are the two sides?" — not "how hot is this muscle?" Absolute skin temperature drifts with the room, the athlete, and the time of day; the left/right gap cancels most of that noise.
  • A persistent gap is the raw signal of one-sided tissue stress.
  • Each side's temperature is the median of its skin pixels — not the average, so a stray reflection or hair can't skew it. Pixels below 15 °C are dropped as background.
  • Left and right are measured independently, then subtracted to give Δ.
temp_side = median( pixel temps where temp ≥ 15°C )
Δ = temp_right − temp_left
Worked example · right hamstring
temp_left = 31.0 °C · temp_right = 31.9 °C
Δ = 31.9 − 31.0 = +0.9 °C The right side runs warmer — but is 0.9 °C meaningful? That depends on what's normal. Step 2.
STEP 2 The thermal signal

What counts as "normal"?

A 0.9 °C gap might be alarming in one athlete and unremarkable in another. COMPASS judges it against a reference — the mean and spread of that region's bilateral difference. The question is whose reference to use.

With no scan history, COMPASS uses a population reference: a mean of 0 °C (symmetry) and a standard deviation of 0.40 °C, drawn from healthy-athlete normative data. As the athlete accumulates their own scans, their personal mean and spread blend in — weighted by how much history exists. This is shrinkage, and it means the system is never naïve early on, yet becomes fully personal with use (Step 8).

SD_eff = sqrt( (n × SD_i^2 + n0 × SD_pop^2) / (n + n0) )
mean_eff = ( n × mean_i + n0 × mean_pop ) / (n + n0)
// n = this region's scans · n0 = 5 · mean_pop = 0 · SD_pop = 0.40 °C
Worked example · right hamstring
Early scans, little personal history → reference is essentially population
reference mean mean_eff = 0 · reference SD SD_eff = 0.40 °C
STEP 3 The thermal signal

Standardised deviation, z

Now the gap becomes comparable across regions and athletes. COMPASS expresses Δ as a number of standard deviations away from normal — how unusual this reading is for this region:

z = | Δ − mean_eff | / SD_eff

A z of 1 means "one SD from symmetry"; a z of 3 means "far outside the normal band". Because it's standardised, the same z carries the same clinical weight on a hamstring, a calf, or a shoulder.

Worked example · right hamstring
z = |0.9 − 0| / 0.40 = 2.25 More than two standard deviations from this region's normal — well into "look closer" territory.
STEP 4 The thermal signal

Thermal-risk score & severity

The deviation z is mapped to a 0–100 Thermal-Risk score (TR) and, in parallel, a clinical severity category. Two anchors set the scale: nothing below 1 SD counts as risk (TR = 0), and 3 SD or beyond is the ceiling (TR = 100).

TR = clamp( (z − 1) / 2 × 100, 0, 100 )

The same z also lands in one of four categories — Clear, Mild, Moderate, Elevated. Both the score and the category are built from the same two anchors (TR = 0 at z = 1, TR = 100 at z = 3, with the midpoint z = 2 splitting Mild from Moderate), so the number and the word can never disagree. Once a region clears Clear, the hotter side is flagged as hyperthermia and the cooler side stays normal. The concern boundary that drives clinical action sits at z > 2 (Moderate). Drag the marker to see a deviation become a severity.

Worked example · right hamstring
TR = (2.25 − 1) / 2 × 100 = 62.5
z = 2.25 falls in 2.0–3.0 → Moderate
right (31.9) hotter than left (31.0) → right hamstring = hyperthermia
STEP 5 The thermal signal

From many regions to one scan score

A full scan covers some 89 regions, each with its own TR. COMPASS does not average them — one badly-stressed region is the clinical signal, and averaging would bury it under dozens of quiet ones. The scan's Thermal-Risk score is the worst region:

Thermal Risk Score = max( TR over all regions )

Coverage is whole-body and fixed: 89 regions — 45 in the anterior view and 44 in the posterior — comprising 43 contralateral pairs plus 3 unpaired median regions (head; head and sacrum on the back). Every region is scored on every scan regardless of the stated injury site, so the per-region detail is preserved for the dashboard while the worst region drives the headline number.

Worked example · this scan
right hamstring TR = 62.5 — the athlete's hottest region today
no other region exceeds it → Thermal Risk Score = 62.5
STEP 6 The recovery signal

The recovery score, R

The second axis comes ready-made. Once per day the athlete's wearable publishes a single 0–100 recovery score via the Spike API, already blending HRV, resting heart rate, sleep, and workload, normalised to that athlete's own history. COMPASS consumes it — it does no readiness modelling of its own.

One guardrail: a freshness gate. If the most recent reading is more than 2 days old, R is treated as unavailable and the readiness axis drops out — COMPASS will not score on stale recovery data.

Worked example · today's wearable
R = 70 The athlete feels recovered — rested, good sleep, workload in range. On its own, this reads "go".

STEP 7 Fusion & action

COMPASS: the zone and the score

With both signals on a 0–100 scale, fusion does two things at once. First it names a zone from a simple 2×2 — each axis split at 50. Then it computes a continuous score by averaging the two signals, with thermal risk counting against the score:

Score = clamp( 50 + 0.5 × (R − TR), 0, 100 )
// readiness (R) and Thermal-Risk (TR) each count half

That simple form is the equal-weight case of a slightly more general one. Each signal is first re-centred to a signed clearance from the 50 line — positive when reassuring, negative when not — and the two are combined with a tunable weight:

r = (R − 50) / 50  ·  tr = (50 − TR) / 50
Score = clamp( 50 + 50 × ( w_R·r + w_TR·tr ), 0, 100 )
// w_R = w_TR = 0.5 (shipped default) → simplifies to 50 + 0.5 × (R − TR)

Note the flip on tr: TR is a risk (high is bad) while the score is a readiness (high is good), so (50 − TR) puts both on the same "higher is better" footing before they combine. Raising w_TR would let tissue asymmetry pull the score down harder; the default treats the two axes symmetrically. Either way the zone is set independently by the quadrant — so an athlete who feels ready but carries real asymmetry still lands in Deceptive regardless of the weighting.

The score is continuous — no per-zone bands or floors — so the two mixed zones overlap on the score axis. Zone and score are always read together. Hover the plane to feel how the two signals combine.

Worked example · the call
Thermal Risk Score = 62.5 (a Moderate hot-spot) · R = 70 (feels recovered)
R ≥ 50 and TR ≥ 50 → Deceptive readiness
Score = 50 + 0.5 × (70 − 62.5) = 50 + 3.75 = ≈ 54 The wearable alone said "70 — go." COMPASS pulls it to a cautious 54 and raises the deceptive-readiness flag pointing straight at the warm hamstring.

The score ranges, and why they overlap

Because the score is continuous while the zone is categorical, the four zones overlap on the 0–100 axis. Safe occupies 50–100, Danger 0–50, and the two mixed zones — Recovery and Deceptive — each span 25–75. So the number alone can't pin the zone: a 54 could be a near-recovered Recovery, a mid Deceptive (our example), or a low Safe. Only the zone label disambiguates — which is why COMPASS always reports the two together. Drag the marker to see which zones a given score can belong to.

What each zone means

The capacity-gap model

The IOC load consensus (Soligard et al. 2016) frames injury risk as the gap between an athlete's load and their capacity: when accumulated load exceeds the capacity to absorb it, risk rises. The four zones map directly onto that gap — combining systemic capacity (the recovery axis) with local tissue capacity (the thermal axis) to characterise exactly what kind of gap, if any, is open.

Soligard T, et al. How much is too much? IOC consensus statement on load in sport and risk of injury. Br J Sports Med. 2016;50(17):1030–1041.

Safe50–100

Recovered body, quiet tissue.

Maintain. Full load; routine cadence; physio elective.

Recovery25–75

Systemic fatigue, tissue clear. Needs rest, not a physio.

Manage load. Reduce volume; target sleep and HRV.

Deceptive Readiness25–75

Feels ready, but a region is stressed — the zone the framework exists to surface. The score sits mid-band by design, so the label is the warning, not the number. (Our worked example lands here.)

Physiotherapy mandatory before any intensity increase. Conservative, region-targeted plan until the flagged tissue settles.

Danger0–50

Fatigued and stressed at once.

Stop & review. Mandatory clinical review before any load plan.

Why the two amber zones are not the same

Conventional systems collapse Recovery and Deceptive Readiness into a single amber. They are opposites in what they ask of the clinician: Recovery needs rest; Deceptive Readiness needs urgent, region-specific physiotherapy. In the Deceptive zone the systemic readiness signal is actively misleading — acting on it without thermal context is the precise injury-risk scenario this framework exists to prevent.

STEP 8 Fusion & action

Learning the athlete's baseline

Our worked example used the population reference because the athlete was new. With every clean scan, that changes. Each region's personal mean and spread gain weight in the blend from Step 2 — weight n / (n + 5) — overtaking the population prior at the fifth scan and approaching the athlete's own variability thereafter. The curve is smooth; there is no moment where the system "switches over".

Worked example · the same hamstring, later
After a month of clean scans, this region's personal SD might settle near 0.30 °C
the same +0.9 °C gap now reads z ≈ 3.0 against a tighter personal norm → a stronger signal, tuned to this athlete

The learning itself is simple. After every scan a background job gathers that region's Δ from every prior scan, adds the new one, and recomputes the personal mean and spread — the standard deviation needs at least two readings before it exists. Only scans with a valid Δ count, so a region that couldn't be measured can't poison the baseline. Those values become the personal reference that future scans are shrunk toward, closing the loop with Step 2. Because shrinkage handles thin histories gracefully, there is no "you need N scans before we score you" gate — every athlete gets a score from scan one.

STEP 9 Fusion & action

Return to play, region by region

The same scan that flags hidden stress governs the comeback. Instead of one verdict for the whole athlete, COMPASS tracks eligibility per region, per side, against that athlete's own norm. A side clears only when its recent history is stable — never on a single good reading.

Worked example · the right hamstring
today's reading on the affected side = Moderate
a concern reading in the last 2 scans → RTP: Pending (NO) It will clear once two consecutive scans come back Clear or Mild — stable normalisation, not a one-off good day.

Because every region is watched on every scan, two patterns the eye misses surface on their own: compensatory overload in a limb the athlete is unconsciously favouring, and undisclosed secondary stress the injury report never mentioned. The athlete is eligible only when every relevant side has cleared.

Watching both limbs at once

Injured athletes compensate by shifting load onto the other limb. For each of the 43 bilateral pairs, COMPASS tracks the left/right delta scan over scan, and the shape of that time series tells the story:

  • Diverging — one side improving while the other worsens: the classic compensatory-load pattern, calling for load redistribution.
  • Converging — both sides normalising together: bilateral symmetry restoring, clearance approaching.
  • Parallel elevation — both sides elevated at once: a systemic or bilateral pattern, not a unilateral injury.

Why this is clinically distinctive

Existing athlete-management platforms report a single composite load or readiness score per athlete. COMPASS instead surfaces a per-region, per-side verdict across all 89 regions — telling a clinician which tissue is still at risk, not merely whether the athlete as a whole is "cleared". The contralateral-delta view makes compensatory overload visible scan by scan, before it becomes a second injury.

Evidence note · scan intervals

The literature supports the direction — higher risk warrants more frequent scanning — but not specific day values. Gómez-Carmona et al. (2020) used daily scanning and cut injuries sharply; Arnaiz-Lastras et al. (2019) used twice-weekly post-match scanning at a >0.4 °C trigger. The exact intervals above are framework design defaults interpolated from that evidence, and clinical leads should treat them as adjustable.

The reasoning engine, and what it cannot do

A multimodal reasoning engine turns each assessment into a plain-language explanation and a tailored plan. It receives the fully computed assessment as a read-only input — it cannot alter scores, zones, or thresholds. All classification stays deterministic and governed by the steps above; the engine's only job is to explain and to recommend.

Clinical practice

Where are they going?

A single zone is a snapshot; clinical urgency comes from movement. The same score, rising or falling, means opposite things — and a score can drift meaningfully within a zone before it ever crosses a boundary. Position tells you what to do today; trajectory tells you how urgently, and whether the current plan is working.

Clinical practice

When a signal is missing

The two streams run on different clocks — a daily wearable summary and a periodic scan — and COMPASS models that gap explicitly rather than papering over it. Each signal is either valid or it is not; there is no silent partial credit.

Thermal · valid for 7 days

The most recent scan is treated as fully valid through day 6 and becomes obsolete on day 7 — a hard cutoff, no gradual decay. When it lapses, thermal scoring is suspended and a scan-overdue flag is raised. Operating without a valid thermal input is itself treated as an elevated-risk state, because silent tissue-stress build-up can no longer be excluded.

Wearable · freshness gate of 2 days

If the latest reading is more than 2 days old, the recovery score is treated as unavailable: the readiness axis drops out and no zone is issued. The output reads "zone assessment unavailable — wearable sync required; last confirmed zone [zone] on [date]." The state is labelled, never silently degraded to a thermal-only guess.

The four combinations of availability each produce a defined behaviour, so no assessment is ever issued with more confidence than the data warrants:

The critical asymmetry

Missing wearable data is the more dangerous gap — not missing thermography. Without the recovery axis, the system loses the ability to detect Deceptive Readiness entirely: an athlete can look and feel ready while tissue stress builds invisibly. COMPASS labels this state explicitly rather than quietly collapsing to a two-zone view.

Reference

Glossary & the numbers behind it

Δ (delta)
The bilateral temperature difference for a region: right minus left, in °C.
Reference (mean_eff, SD_eff)
The mean and spread the gap is judged against — the athlete's personal stats shrunk toward the population.
z (standardised deviation)
How many standard deviations the gap sits from normal. Comparable across regions and athletes.
TR (Thermal-Risk score)
The 0–100 risk from the thermal axis. The scan's TR is the worst region's TR.
Severity
The clinical category of a region: Clear, Mild, Moderate, Elevated. Moderate (z > 2) is the concern boundary.
R (recovery score)
The 0–100 systemic recovery figure from the wearable, via Spike.
Zone
One of Safe, Recovery, Deceptive Readiness, Danger — set by R and TR against a 50/50 split.
COMPASS score
The continuous 0–100 readiness number from the equal-weight fusion of R and TR.
Anchors (z = 1, z = 3)
The two fixed deviations that pin the scale: TR = 0 at z = 1, TR = 100 at z = 3. The severity bands share them, so the score and the category cannot disagree.
Shrinkage
Blending a thin personal history toward the stable population reference, so early scans are never trusted too much — and there is no minimum-scan gate.
RTP clearance
Per-side return-to-play status: YES, NO (pending), or N/A (insufficient history).

The tunable constants, and their defaults

SD_pop = 0.40°C
Population SD of bilateral difference — the no-history reference.
mean_pop = 0
Population mean — perfect bilateral symmetry.
n0 = 5
Shrinkage prior strength; personal & population reach parity at 5 scans.
z = 1 → 3
Severity anchors: TR = 0 at 1 SD, TR = 100 at 3 SD.
weight = 0.5
Equal weighting of R and TR in the COMPASS score.
freshness = 2 d
Wearable data older than this drops the readiness axis.
thermal valid = 7 d
A scan stays valid through day 6; on day 7 thermal scoring is suspended.
bg filter = 15°C
Pixels colder than this are treated as room, not body, and dropped.

Every value here is a deliberate, adjustable dial — set from clinical reasoning, not a fact of nature — and can be re-tuned as data accrues without changing any of the logic above.

Appendix A

Standardised scan protocol

A personal baseline is meaningful only if every scan is captured the same way, so a temperature change reflects the athlete — not the room.

  • Environment. Climate-controlled, 18–25 °C, stable and draught-free; no direct sun or radiant heat on the athlete.
  • Acclimatisation. 10–15 minutes of skin equilibration in the scan room before capture, region uncovered.
  • Preparation. Clean, dry skin; no creams, tape, or strapping on the region; remove jewellery; hair clear of the neck.
  • Pre-scan restrictions. No exercise, hot/cold therapy, massage, or shower in the window before scanning.
  • Timing. Scan before training where possible; keep the same time-of-day relative to activity — never compare a morning scan to an afternoon one.
  • Coverage. Anterior and posterior full-body views; both sides of every bilateral region.

Capture setup, per session

Camera geometry must be documented and held constant; changing distance, angle, emissivity, or scale invalidates direct comparison with prior sessions. Setup follows IACT and ATA clinical guidance.

  • Distance. 1.0 m fixed; record it — distance shifts pixel density and apparent temperature.
  • Angle. 90° perpendicular in both planes; oblique angles lower readings at the periphery (cosine emission) and corrupt bilateral comparison.
  • Emissivity. 0.98 — human skin across the LWIR band, independent of skin tone.
  • Scale. Fixed min/max, auto-scale disabled, for longitudinal comparability.
  • Image set. Anterior + posterior full body, plus close-ups of bilateral regions of interest.
  • Warm-up. Per manufacturer spec; uncooled sensors need thermal equilibration before readings are stable.
